How I Think

Principles that guide how I work.

A practical approach to product decisions, grounded in user behaviour and measurable outcomes.

Start with the problem

I try to understand where users actually run into friction before settling on a solution. Most feature requests trace back to a workflow gap, not a missing feature.

Go beyond the first answer

The first version of a problem statement is usually the symptom. I lean on data and direct user input to figure out what's actually driving it.

Design for real usage

The happy path matters, but trust gets built or broken at the edges. I plan for the moments where systems fail or behave in ways users don't expect.

Define success before building

Every feature should ship with a clear way to tell whether it worked. Agreeing on that up front keeps the team honest after launch.

Key Product Decisions

A few calls I made and what they cost.

Every shipped product is a stack of decisions, and each one left something on the table. Here's the reasoning behind three of them.

Razorpay over a global gateway

Why

Card success rates on Indian corporate cards were noticeably higher, settlements were faster, and reconciliation was cleaner for finance. Since the customers were Indian enterprises, we optimised for their payment behaviour rather than international coverage.

Trade-off

Running on a single provider introduced concentration risk. We accepted that for v1 and planned to add a fallback once volume justified the extra moving parts.

Google Maps for the fare breakdown

Why

Users didn't need a different price; they needed to trust the one they were seeing. Showing distance and ETA from a familiar source reframed the number from "our estimate" to "the standard," and drop-offs at the fare screen fell shortly after.

Trade-off

Added per-call cost and a third-party dependency on a high-traffic screen. Caching softened the bill, and the trust gain made the spend defensible.

Rule-based approvals over free-form email

Why

Email approvals had no structure or visibility, and bookings stalled for days. A rules engine — approvers, thresholds, reminders — pulled turnaround down to hours and gave finance a live view of committed spend.

Trade-off

We lost the flexibility of informal exceptions. To compensate, we added an explicit manager override path and kept the defaults strict so exceptions stayed visible.

Journey

Frontend → Product

From frontend engineering to product management.

I started as a frontend engineer. Most of my time went into figuring out why a flow that worked on paper broke in production — bad network, stale state, an edge case nobody mentioned in the spec.

That pulled me into product conversations earlier and earlier — what we were actually solving, what we were willing to cut, what "done" looked like. Eventually that part became the work.

Today I focus on getting the problem clear, keeping the team aligned on what matters, and shipping things that hold up once real users get to them.
